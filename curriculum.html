Biography (as of May 2014)

Mauricio Carneiro was born in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil on February 11, 1980. His interest in computer science began at an early age, and he was soon enrolled in a technical high school with a focus on computer science. During his days as an undergraduate at Pontifical Catholic University of Rio de Janeiro (PUC-RIO), nationally regarded as the best computer engineering university in Brazil, he competed in multiple computer science contests. Most notably, he represented PUC-RIO for three years at the International Collegiate Programming Contest, the most competitive international tournament for computer programming for college students. He won South American Champion in 2003, and at the world finals was awarded the Upsilon pi Epsilon (UPE) Programming Excellence. The UPE is a society that promotes academic excellence in computing and information disciplines.

He won multiple scholarships to pursue research in computer graphics and advanced algorithms throughout his time at PUC-RIO. In 2003 he was awarded a scholarship from the Brazilian Research Council to work as a junior researcher in molecular epidemiology. It was during this time that he developed an interest in computational biology, and decided to pursue a Masters of Science in molecular biology at Oswaldo Cruz Foundation and Institute (Rio de Janeiro), with the aid of a full scholarship from the Brazilian Research Council. With this training under his belt, and with a full commitment to applying his computational knowledge to the growing field of computation genomics, he applied, and was accepted, into the PhD program at Harvard University's Department of Organismic and Evolutionary Biology, with acclaimed geneticist Dr. Daniel Hartl.

During his graduate training, Dr. Carneiro drew on his computational background to develop a method for understanding the dynamics of drug resistance in the malaria parasite. This culminated in a highly influential paper published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. In addition, he worked on the annotation and analysis of the first Drosophila mauritiana genome, which was published in Genome Biology and Evolution. Finally, he collaborated with noted Harvard mathematics professor Cliff Taubes to develop a mathematical model for the evolution of gene networks. This work was published in the journal BMC Evolutionary Biology.

As his formal education drew to an end, Dr. Carneiro was recruited to apply his expertise at the Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard. The Broad Institute is arguably the premier genomics research center in the world, and a powerful driver of innovation in the field. Dr. Carneiro joined the Medical and Population Genetics Program at the Broad, where he began his career as a Computational Biologist.

Within his first year at the Broad Institute, Dr. Carneiro made major contributions to the field of DNA sequence analysis, especially as it applied to disease discovery. For example, his data compression algorithm enabled the analysis of tens of thousands of individuals simultaneously, which allowed disease researchers to find very rare (and previously missed) causal mutations for complex diseases, such as diabetes and schizophrenia. Before this innovation, the analysis was limited to only a few hundred individuals at a time, and left out crucial information about these individuals' genomes. This compression algorithm is now the gold standard for large-scale DNA sequence analysis, and has been patented under US PCT Patent US13/31429 "Systems and methods for reducing representations of genome sequencing data". Dr. Carneiro also identified an unmet need for replication and validation of large-scale data sets in disease studies. To address this gap, he created a study design, sequencing protocol and computational algorithm that significantly reduces the cost of sequencing a large number of individuals. This procedure is now being successfully used at the Broad Institute and is currently under patent processing.

Based on his outstanding performance, Dr. Carneiro was quickly promoted to a leadership role, and in his second year at the Broad, became Group Lead in Computational Technology Development. In this new role, Dr. Carneiro has contributed to major advances in DNA sequencing such as the new analytical methods for exome sequencing and small target resequencing studies, again reducing the cost of large sequencing projects and further enabling the research of complex diseases. His group has also played an important role disseminating information on how to use and apply computational analytics to DNA sequencing data including but not limited to human data. His group organized several workshops and distributed videotaped lectures for free in the Broad Institute's website.

During his time at the Broad Institute, Dr. Carneiro has been invited to speak at the top conferences in his field, including twice at the cornerstone conference in genome sequencing technology, Advances in Genome Biology and Technology (AGBT). Dr. Carneiro has also published six highly cited papers in top journals, including two in Nature, one in Nature Communications, as well as papers in BMC Genomics, Current Protocols in Bioinformatics, and Genome Biology. The last paper in Genome Biology, entitled "The advantages of SMRT sequencing" was a collaboration between Dr. Carneiro and Nobel Laureate in Physiology or Medicine, Sir Richard Roberts. The majority of Dr. Carneiro's work at the Broad has been, and continues to be, compiled into his software compendium known as the Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK), which is freely available to the scientific community. The GATK quickly became the worldwide industry standard for DNA sequencing data analysis. The GATK surpassed the 100 million run mark in August 2013, and has been used by close to 10,000 researchers and clinicians, from academic and institutions and commercial companies alike. The numbers keep growing, and everyday, at least twenty to thirty new users join the GATK support forum to download the software, read documentation, and ask questions about the GATK analysis tools and methods. This is a testament to the fact that the GATK is the most widely used software package for analysis of genome information, not only of humans, but of a broad range of organisms, by researchers across the world.
